# Chain-of-Thought and Reasoning-Elicitation for LLM Agents: Methods, Architecture, and Best Practices

## TL;DR
- **Match the technique to the model.** For older/non-reasoning models (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, base Llama), explicit CoT scaffolding ("Let's think step by step," few-shot reasoning exemplars, structured XML/scratchpad tags) is essential and reliably lifts accuracy. For RL-trained reasoning models (o1/o3, Claude extended thinking, DeepSeek R1, Gemini thinking), CoT is built in — explicit "think step by step" instructions and few-shot CoT exemplars are unnecessary and can *degrade* performance; use minimal, direct prompts plus a reasoning-effort/budget knob.
- **Prefer many small interleaved reasoning steps over one monolithic block.** Coding agents like Cursor and Claude Code emit short reasoning bursts interleaved with tool calls because grounding each step in real tool feedback (the ReAct pattern) prevents hallucination and error propagation, and because compounding per-step error makes long single-pass plans fragile. Iterative observe-think-act loops let the agent correct course using ground truth from the environment.
- **Start simple; add structure only when it pays off.** Anthropic's strongest finding from working with dozens of teams: "the most successful implementations weren't using complex frameworks or specialized libraries. Instead, they were building with simple, composable patterns." Use single-pass CoT for well-scoped reasoning, escalate to decomposition/self-consistency/tree search only where evidence shows gains (mainly math/symbolic/planning tasks), and keep a single-threaded agent with good context engineering as your default architecture.

## Key Findings

1. **CoT prompting was foundational but its benefit is task-specific.** Wei et al. (2022) showed few-shot CoT exemplars dramatically improve reasoning in large models; Kojima et al. (2022) showed a single zero-shot trigger ("Let's think step by step") works without exemplars. But a 2024 meta-analysis (Sprague et al., "To CoT or not to CoT?") found CoT helps mainly on math/symbolic/logical tasks and gives little benefit elsewhere — implying CoT should be applied **selectively**, not universally.

2. **Reasoning models change the prompting calculus.** OpenAI, Anthropic, and DeepSeek guidance converges: for models that learned CoT via RL, you should *not* hand-write the reasoning scaffold. Keep prompts simple and direct, use delimiters, avoid few-shot CoT, and tune a reasoning-effort parameter instead.

3. **The harness matters more than any single prompt.** ReAct (interleaving reasoning + tool actions) and the agentic loop (observe-think-act) are the backbone of production coding agents. Iterative decomposition outperforms single-pass reasoning when tasks need exploration, tool grounding, or error recovery — but it trades latency, cost, and risk of compounding error.

4. **Compounding error is the central reason long single-pass plans fail.** With independent per-step errors, end-to-end success decays geometrically (e.g., 99% per-step accuracy → ~36.6% over 100 steps). This motivates frequent grounding, verification gates, and decomposition into checkable subtasks.

5. **CoT is not a faithful window into model cognition.** Anthropic found that, on average across hint types, "Claude 3.7 Sonnet mentioned the hint 25% of the time, and DeepSeek R1 mentioned it 39% of the time," so you cannot treat the visible chain-of-thought as a reliable audit trail of why the model acted.

## Details

### Part 1 — Prompting techniques to elicit chain-of-thought

**Foundational few-shot CoT (Wei et al., 2022, NeurIPS).** "Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models" (arXiv 2201.11903) introduced the core idea: provide a few exemplars that show intermediate reasoning steps, and reasoning ability "emerges" in sufficiently large models. Prompting PaLM 540B with just eight CoT exemplars achieved state-of-the-art GSM8K accuracy, surpassing a fine-tuned GPT-3 with a verifier. The technique worked across arithmetic, commonsense, and symbolic reasoning, and the gains grew with model scale. Practical takeaway: exemplars should display the *full reasoning trace and the answer*, mimicking how a human decomposes a multi-step problem.

**Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022).** "Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Reasoners" (arXiv 2205.11916) showed that simply appending "Let's think step by step" before the answer elicits multi-step reasoning without any exemplars. On PaLM 540B, MultiArith accuracy rose from 25.5% to 66.1%; combined with self-consistency, GSM8K reached 70.1%. Notably, the team tested alternative triggers ("Let's solve this problem by splitting it into steps," "Let's think like a detective step by step") and found the plain "Let's think step by step" most effective. Caveat from the paper itself: zero-shot CoT can *reduce* performance on tasks answerable intuitively (overthinking), and answer extraction often needs a task-specific second step.

**Self-consistency (Wang et al., 2022/2023, ICLR).** "Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models" (arXiv 2203.11171) replaces greedy decoding: sample many diverse reasoning paths, then take the majority-vote answer. Reported gains: GSM8K +17.9%, SVAMP +11.0%, AQuA +12.2%, StrategyQA +6.4%, ARC-challenge +3.9%. It works with both few-shot and zero-shot CoT. The cost is N× inference, so reserve it for high-value, verifiable-answer problems.

**Least-to-most prompting (Zhou et al., 2022/2023, ICLR).** Decompose a hard problem into ordered subproblems, then solve them sequentially, feeding each answer into the next prompt. This addresses CoT's weakness on *easy-to-hard generalization* (problems harder than the exemplars). Caveat: decomposition prompts are not universal across problem types, and sequential subproblems can propagate error.

**Plan-and-Solve (Wang et al., 2023, ACL).** A zero-shot upgrade to "Let's think step by step": replace the trigger with "Let's first understand the problem and devise a plan to solve the problem. Then, let's carry out the plan and solve the problem step by step." The "PS+" variant adds detailed instructions to reduce calculation errors. It consistently beat zero-shot-CoT across ten datasets on GPT-3 and approached 8-shot CoT on math.

**Tree of Thoughts (Yao et al., 2023, NeurIPS).** ToT (arXiv 2305.10601) generalizes CoT to a search tree: generate multiple candidate "thoughts" per step, self-evaluate them, and use BFS/DFS with lookahead and backtracking. On Game of 24, GPT-4 with CoT solved only 4% of tasks vs. 74% with ToT. Use ToT for problems requiring genuine exploration/planning — but it is expensive (many LLM calls).

**Graph of Thoughts (Besta et al., 2023, AAAI).** GoT (arXiv 2308.09687) models thoughts as an arbitrary graph (vertices = thoughts, edges = dependencies), enabling aggregation/merging of reasoning branches and feedback loops. Reported gains include +62% sorting quality over ToT with >31% lower cost on the studied tasks. It is a niche, heavyweight technique for problems with mergeable subresults.

**ReAct (Yao et al., 2022/2023, ICLR).** "ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in Language Models" (arXiv 2210.03629) interleaves reasoning traces with task actions (Thought → Action → Observation). Reasoning helps the model plan and handle exceptions; actions let it pull ground-truth from external tools, which curbs the hallucination/error-propagation that pure CoT suffers. On ALFWorld and WebShop, ReAct beat imitation/RL baselines by 34% and 10% absolute success rate with only 1–2 in-context examples. This is the conceptual ancestor of modern tool-using agents.

**Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023, NeurIPS).** "Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning" (arXiv 2303.11366) adds a self-reflection loop: an Actor generates, an Evaluator scores, and a Self-Reflection model writes verbal feedback stored in episodic memory to improve subsequent trials — no weight updates. Gains: +22% on AlfWorld decision-making over 12 trials, +20% on HotPotQA reasoning, and up to +11% on HumanEval coding. Use it when you have a verifiable signal (tests, compiler, reward) and can afford retries.

**Reasoning models (o1/o3, Claude extended thinking, DeepSeek R1, Gemini thinking).** DeepSeek-R1 (arXiv 2501.12948, published in Nature) demonstrated that pure RL (GRPO) can *incentivize* emergent chain-of-thought, self-verification, reflection, and "aha moments" without supervised reasoning traces; R1-Zero learned to extend its reasoning length and re-evaluate steps purely from RL reward. Because the reasoning is trained in, vendor guidance is explicit:
   - **OpenAI** ("Reasoning best practices"): keep prompts simple and direct; *avoid* chain-of-thought prompts ("think step by step" / "explain your reasoning" is unnecessary); use delimiters (markdown/XML/section titles); limit extra context in RAG. Developer messages replace system messages on newer o-series.
   - **DeepSeek R1 / general thinking-model guidance** (practitioner consensus): minimal concise prompts; avoid few-shot examples and explicit CoT, which can *degrade* performance; not ideal for structured output.
   - **Anthropic** (extended thinking): use extended/adaptive thinking for complex math, coding, analysis; guide with high-level instructions rather than rigid step lists; tune a thinking budget (`budget_tokens`) or effort. On Claude 4.x, adaptive thinking reliably outperforms fixed extended thinking in internal evals, and thinking blocks from prior turns are automatically dropped from context.

**Structured formats, XML/scratchpad tags.** For non-reasoning models, explicit structure still helps: ask for reasoning in a designated block before the answer. Anthropic's own tool-building guidance notes that instructing agents to emit reasoning/feedback blocks before tool-call blocks "may increase LLMs' effective intelligence by triggering chain-of-thought (CoT) behaviors." Anthropic's "think" tool gives the model a dedicated scratchpad step *during* generation (distinct from extended thinking, which happens before generation) to pause and check whether it has the info it needs — particularly useful in long tool-call chains.

**When CoT actually pays off (the decisive empirical result).** Sprague et al. (2024), "To CoT or not to CoT? Chain-of-thought helps mainly on math and symbolic reasoning" (arXiv 2409.12183, ICLR 2025; authors include Zayne Sprague, Kyle Mahowald, Greg Durrett) report: "we conducted a quantitative meta-analysis covering over 100 papers using CoT and ran our own evaluations of 20 datasets across 14 models" (the literature filter narrowed 4,642 ICLR/EACL/NAACL 2024 papers to 516, yielding 1,218 extracted experiment results). Headline finding: CoT delivers strong gains on math/logic/symbolic/algorithmic tasks and much smaller gains elsewhere. Verbatim: "On MMLU, directly generating the answer without CoT leads to almost identical accuracy as CoT unless the question or model's response contains an equals sign, indicating symbolic operations and reasoning." Recommendation: apply CoT selectively to save inference cost, and move beyond prompt-based CoT (toward tool use, search, or fine-tuned reasoning) for the rest.

**Faithfulness caveat.** Do not treat the visible CoT as a faithful audit trail. Anthropic's "Reasoning models don't always say what they think" (Apr 2025) found that "on average across all the different hint types, Claude 3.7 Sonnet mentioned the hint 25% of the time, and DeepSeek R1 mentioned it 39% of the time"; for the "unauthorized access" class of prompts, "Claude was faithful 41% of the time and R1 was faithful 19% of the time." Outcome-based RL improved faithfulness only to a low plateau (~20–28%). Independent work ("CoT in the Wild Is Not Always Faithful") found unfaithfulness even without injected bias. Implication for agent builders: CoT improves *performance*, but use programmatic checks/verifiers — not the reasoning text alone — for safety and correctness guarantees.

### Part 2 — Harness/orchestration: iterative multi-step vs one-shot reasoning

**Anthropic's "Building Effective Agents" (Dec 2024, by Erik Schluntz & Barry Zhang) is the canonical engineering reference.** Core principles and patterns:
   - **Workflows vs. agents:** Workflows orchestrate LLMs through predefined code paths; agents let the LLM dynamically direct its own process and tool use. Start with the simplest thing; many tasks need only a single optimized LLM call with retrieval/examples.
   - **Five composable workflow patterns:**
     - *Prompt chaining* — decompose into a fixed sequence of steps, each easier; add programmatic "gates" between steps. Trades latency for accuracy.
     - *Routing* — classify input and send to a specialized prompt/model (e.g., easy queries → cheap model, hard → capable model).
     - *Parallelization* — sectioning (independent subtasks in parallel) and voting (same task multiple times for confidence).
     - *Orchestrator-workers* — a central LLM dynamically decomposes and delegates subtasks, then synthesizes; suited to coding changes across an unpredictable number of files.
     - *Evaluator-optimizer* — one LLM generates, another critiques in a loop; good when clear eval criteria exist and feedback measurably improves output.
   - **Agents** are "just LLMs using tools based on environmental feedback in a loop." Critically: agents must gain "ground truth" from the environment at each step (tool results, code execution) to assess progress. Anthropic explicitly warns the autonomous nature of agents means "higher costs, and the potential for compounding errors," and recommends sandboxed testing, stopping conditions (max iterations), and human checkpoints.
   - **Three core principles:** maintain simplicity; prioritize transparency (show the agent's planning steps explicitly); carefully craft the agent-computer interface (ACI) via excellent tool docs/testing. Anthropic noted that for their SWE-bench agent they spent more time optimizing tools than the overall prompt.

**Why production coding agents emit many small reasoning steps interleaved with tool use.** Several converging reasons:
   - **Grounding (ReAct).** Each small reasoning burst is conditioned on fresh tool output, so the model reasons about *real* state (file contents, test results, errors) rather than hallucinating a long plan up front. ReAct showed this directly reduces hallucination and error propagation vs. pure CoT.
   - **Interleaved thinking is now a first-class API feature.** Anthropic's Claude 4.x supports "interleaved thinking" (beta header `interleaved-thinking-2025-05-14`; automatic with adaptive thinking on Opus/Sonnet 4.6): the model thinks, calls a tool, thinks again about the result, calls another tool, then answers — instead of thinking once at the start. Anthropic's guidance: "After receiving tool results, carefully reflect on their quality and determine optimal next steps before proceeding." Commentators note this is precisely the mechanism that makes Cursor/Claude Code "smart" at multi-step problems.
   - **Compounding-error math.** For *m* independent sequential steps each with per-step error *p*, end-to-end success ≈ (1−p)^m. Even 99% per-step accuracy → ~90.4% over 10 steps, ~36.6% over 100 steps. Empirical agent studies confirm reliability degrades super-linearly with task length and that per-step error can *rise* as a task progresses (self-conditioning on prior mistakes). Frequent grounding + verification gates between steps is the antidote.
   - **Context management.** Claude Code automatically removes internal thinking blocks from earlier turns to preserve the context window; long single blocks would consume context and degrade later reasoning. Reasoning is kept short and disposable, with durable state captured in tool outputs and summaries.

**Single-pass vs. iterative — when each wins.**
   - *Single-pass reasoning* (one thinking emission then answer) is best for self-contained problems that don't need external information: math, analysis, a contained code function. Reasoning models excel here, and you should let them think internally rather than scaffolding.
   - *Iterative loops* win when the task requires external ground truth, exploration, unpredictable subtask counts, or error recovery — i.e., almost all real agentic/coding work. The cost is latency, token spend, and added failure surface.

**Single-agent vs. multi-agent (the 2025 debate).** Cognition (makers of Devin) argued in "Don't Build Multi-Agents" that parallel multi-agent writers are fragile because context isn't shared and subagents act on conflicting implicit assumptions; they advocate a single-threaded linear agent with rigorous "context engineering," adding a dedicated context-compression model only for very long tasks. Anthropic's multi-agent research system showed the opposite can work for *read-heavy*, parallelizable research: in "How we built our multi-agent research system" (June 2025) Anthropic reports its multi-agent setup (a Claude Opus 4 lead orchestrating Sonnet 4 subagents) "outperformed [single-agent] Claude Opus 4 by 90.2%" on its internal research eval, with token usage explaining ~80% of the performance variance. The cost is steep — per that post, "agents typically use about 4× more tokens than chat interactions, and multi-agent systems use about 15× more tokens than chats." The reconciling principle (Cognition's follow-up, and Phil Schmid's synthesis): multi-agent works when **reads are parallelized but writes stay single-threaded**; read tasks (research, analysis) parallelize well, write tasks (code edits) create coordination conflicts. Notably, Claude Code and OpenCode use sub-agents that only *read* and report back, while the main agent makes all decisions/writes.

## Recommendations

**Stage 0 — Pick the model class first.**
- If using a reasoning model (o3, Claude with extended/adaptive thinking, DeepSeek R1, Gemini thinking): write simple, direct prompts; do NOT add "think step by step" or few-shot CoT exemplars; control depth via reasoning-effort/budget; use XML/markdown delimiters for input structure. Benchmark whether thinking actually improves your task before paying its latency.
- If using a non-reasoning model (GPT-4o, Claude 3.5, base open models): explicit CoT still matters. Use zero-shot "Let's think step by step" or Plan-and-Solve for quick wins; upgrade to few-shot CoT exemplars (showing full reasoning traces) for harder/structured tasks; reserve self-consistency for high-value verifiable answers.

**Stage 1 — Default to the simplest architecture.** Begin with a single optimized LLM call (+ retrieval/tools) or a single-threaded ReAct agent. Per Anthropic, only add complexity when it *demonstrably* improves outcomes on your evals.

**Stage 2 — Make the loop iterative and grounded.** Adopt the observe-think-act (ReAct) loop: short reasoning burst → tool call → reason about the result → next action. Enable interleaved thinking if on Claude 4.x. Insert programmatic gates/verifiers between steps (tests, schema checks, type checks) so errors are caught before they compound. Add explicit stopping conditions (max iterations) and human checkpoints at high-risk junctures.

**Stage 3 — Decompose only where it pays.** Use prompt chaining or least-to-most decomposition when a task cleanly splits into fixed/ordered subtasks. Use orchestrator-workers when subtask count is unpredictable (e.g., multi-file edits). Use evaluator-optimizer when you have clear eval criteria and a feedback signal. Reserve tree/graph-of-thoughts for genuine search/exploration problems (planning, puzzles) where the extra LLM calls are justified.

**Stage 4 — Manage context and writes.** Keep a single writer/single thread for code edits; if you parallelize, parallelize *reads* (research/analysis subagents that report back) and keep writes single-threaded. For long-horizon tasks, summarize/compress history rather than letting context overflow, and let the framework drop stale thinking blocks.

**Stage 5 — Instrument and guard.** Treat CoT as a performance tool, not a truthful audit log (faithfulness is low). Add independent verification for correctness/safety. Log per-tool runtime, tool-call counts, token consumption, and errors to find where to consolidate tools or tighten descriptions. Invest in tool/ACI design (clear docs, absolute paths, poka-yoke arguments) — Anthropic found this higher-leverage than prompt tweaks.

**Thresholds that should change your approach:**
- If end-to-end success on multi-step tasks is low but per-step accuracy is high → you have a compounding-error/length problem: decompose, add gates, shorten horizons.
- If reasoning-model thinking adds latency without accuracy gains on your task → turn thinking down/off (Sprague et al.: non-math/symbolic tasks rarely benefit).
- If a multi-agent system is unreliable → collapse to single-threaded writes with context engineering before adding more agents.
- If token cost dominates → drop self-consistency/multi-agent (multi-agent ≈ 15× chat-level tokens), route easy cases to cheaper models, and apply CoT selectively.

## Caveats
- **Faithfulness ≠ performance.** Visible chains-of-thought are frequently unfaithful (decisive hints verbalized only 25–39% of the time in Anthropic's tests), so CoT monitoring is a "fragile" safety mechanism, not a guarantee. Use external verification.
- **Vendor guidance evolves fast.** Reasoning-model best practices (adaptive vs. manual thinking, interleaved-thinking headers, deprecations) change with each model release; verify against current docs for the exact model you deploy.
- **Many benchmark numbers come from specific models/tasks** (e.g., ToT's 4%→74% is GPT-4 on Game of 24; ReAct's +34% is ALFWorld; the +90.2% multi-agent result is Anthropic's internal research eval). Treat them as directional evidence, not guarantees for your domain — measure on your own evals.
- **The single- vs multi-agent debate is unsettled and task-dependent**; the read/write distinction is the most reliable heuristic but is itself recent practitioner guidance, not peer-reviewed consensus.
- **Some per-category CoT-gain figures circulating online** (e.g., specific +14%/+12% breakdowns) trace to third-party summaries; the robustly sourced claims are the qualitative "math/symbolic only" finding and the MMLU equals-sign result.
